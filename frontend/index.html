Getting Started

Loupe supports multiple instrument modes — Microscope, Deflectometry, and Fringe Analysis — selectable from the mode dropdown in the top bar. Microscope is the default mode and provides precision measurement tools for camera or imported images.

Basic workflow
1Freeze a frame using the ❄ Live button, or drag and drop an image onto the viewer.
2Calibrate the image: press C, click two points of known distance, enter the value in mm.
3Select a measurement tool from the Measure ▾ flyout in the toolbar, or press its shortcut key. Click points on the image to place measurements. Some tools show extra options (e.g. point order) in the options row above the flyout.
4Export results as CSV, PDF, or annotated PNG from the Overlay menu.
Tips
Hover over the image with a measurement tool active — the cursor snaps to the nearest detected edge automatically. Hold Alt to bypass snapping and place a point exactly where you click.
Use ? at any time to open this documentation.
Sessions auto-save to your browser every 30 seconds. Press S to save manually as a JSON file you can reload later.

How It Works

Phase-shifting deflectometry for measuring the surface form of specular (mirror-like, reflective) parts.

Principle

An iPad displays sinusoidal fringe patterns. The camera observes their reflection off the part surface. Distortions in the reflected fringes encode the surface slope, which is integrated (Frankot-Chellappa) to recover surface height. Eight phase-shifted captures per direction suppress LCD harmonic artifacts.

iPad connection

The iPad runs a companion page (deflectometry-screen.html) that connects to Loupe via WebSocket. Open the URL shown in the status badge on the iPad's browser. Connection is automatic — the badge turns green when linked.

Workspace layout

The workspace has two columns. The left column shows the live camera preview, status badges, setup buttons, calibration, and settings. The right column has an action bar with processing controls and tabbed results below.

Deflectometry only works on specular (mirror-like) surfaces. Ground, brushed, or matte surfaces scatter the reflected fringes and will not produce usable results. A dark enclosure with minimal internal reflections dramatically improves results.

Setup

Prepare the system before measuring a part. Both steps are in the left column under Setup.

Flat Field

Captures frames with the display off to measure ambient light and sensor noise. This is subtracted from all subsequent captures. Re-run if lighting conditions change.

Baseline

Place a flat reference mirror in the field of view, then click Baseline. Captures 8-step phase-shifted fringes in both X and Y directions. This establishes the reference surface that part measurements are compared against.

Use an optical flat or first-surface mirror for the baseline. The quality of your reference directly limits measurement accuracy.

Sphere Calibration

Converts the computed phase map from arbitrary phase units to physical surface height (micrometres).

How to calibrate
1Place a precision sphere of known radius in the field of view.
2Capture it as a part (Capture Part + Compute).
3Click Sphere Calibration in the left column and enter the sphere radius.

Loupe fits a paraboloid to the measured phase and derives a phase-to-height scale factor from the known sphere geometry. After calibration, all results are reported in micrometres.

Sphere requirements

The sphere must be specular — chrome steel or ceramic balls work well. Use DIN 5401 grade G28 or better (G28 = 0.7 µm form tolerance). The sphere should fill a reasonable portion of the field of view for a good fit.

How It Works

Monochromatic interferometric fringe analysis for quantitative surface flatness measurement.

Principle

When a flat optical reference (an optical flat) is placed on a lapped surface and illuminated with monochromatic light, interference fringes form. Each fringe represents a contour of constant air gap, spaced at half the wavelength of light (~295 nm for sodium). Straight, evenly spaced fringes indicate a flat surface; curved or irregularly spaced fringes indicate surface errors.

Digital analysis

Loupe digitizes this process: a single photograph of the interferogram is analyzed using 2D Fourier transform (DFT) phase extraction. The carrier frequency (the regular fringe spacing) is isolated in the frequency domain, and the surface-error phase is extracted from the sideband. This phase is then unwrapped, decomposed into Zernike polynomials, and converted to physical height using the known wavelength.

What you need

A monochromatic light source (sodium lamp, HeNe laser, or narrow-band LED), an optical flat, the part to be measured, and a camera to photograph the fringes. No iPad or multi-frame capture is required, unlike deflectometry.

Setup

Prepare the system before analyzing an interferogram.

Wavelength

Select the correct wavelength for your light source from the dropdown. Common choices: Sodium (589 nm), HeNe laser (632.8 nm), or Green LED (532 nm). If your source is different, select Custom and enter the wavelength in nanometers. The wavelength determines the physical height conversion: height = phase × λ / (4π).

Mask threshold

The mask threshold slider controls how aggressively the auto-masking rejects low-contrast pixels. Increase it if background noise is contaminating the results; decrease it if valid fringe areas are being excluded. Default is 15%.

Focus quality

The focus quality bar shows a real-time sharpness score (0-100) for the camera preview. Higher is better. Adjust focus and lighting until the score is above 60 before capturing.

Capture & Analyze

How to acquire and analyze an interferogram.

From camera

Click Freeze & Analyze to capture the current camera frame and run the full analysis pipeline. The camera frame is frozen, converted to grayscale, and processed through DFT phase extraction, Zernike fitting, and surface rendering.

From file

Drag and drop any image file (PNG, JPEG, TIFF) onto the drop zone in the left column. This is useful for analyzing saved interferograms or images from other cameras.

Pipeline
1Auto-mask: pixels with low fringe contrast are excluded.
22D FFT extracts the carrier frequency and isolates the surface-error sideband.
3Phase unwrapping resolves 2π ambiguities into a continuous surface.
4Zernike polynomial fitting (36 terms) decomposes the surface into known aberration modes.
5Selected terms are subtracted and the residual is converted to physical height.
Polygon mask

Click Edit Mask to define analysis boundaries using Microscope mode's drawing tools. Draw area polygons to outline the part. Right-click any polygon to toggle between Punch (include) and Die (exclude). Click Apply Mask when done — the mask persists across re-analyses while the camera stays live. Click Clear Mask to remove all mask regions.

Wavefront Averaging

Reduce random noise by averaging multiple captures of the same surface.

How to average

Click Add to Average after each analysis to accumulate captures. The averaged wavefront is recomputed after each addition. The capture log shows all included captures with their individual RMS values.

Automatic rejection

Captures with unusually high RMS (indicating vibration, air bubbles, or a bumped flat) are automatically rejected. The threshold is configurable: by default, a capture is rejected if its RMS exceeds 3× the running average. Rejected captures appear with a red ✗ in the capture log. Click any entry in the log to manually include or exclude it from the average.

Zernike Subtraction

Control which surface error modes are removed from the measurement.

Why subtract?

Zernike polynomials describe common optical aberrations. Subtracting certain terms isolates the surface errors you care about. For example, tilt (Z2, Z3) is caused by the optical flat not being perfectly parallel to the surface; removing it shows only the actual surface shape error.

Results & Tabs

Understanding the output of a fringe analysis.

Summary bar

Shows PV (peak-to-valley) and RMS in both waves (λ) and nanometers (nm). PV is the total range of surface error; RMS is the statistical spread. A surface with PV < 1λ is considered "optically flat" for many applications.

Surface Map tab

False-color height map: red = high, blue = low (relative). The colormap is symmetric around zero after Zernike subtraction. Masked pixels appear black. Scroll to zoom, drag to pan.

3D View tab

Interactive 3D surface visualization. Drag to rotate, scroll to zoom, right-drag to pan. The Z exaggeration slider amplifies the height for visibility.

Zernike tab

Bar chart of all 36 fitted Zernike coefficients in units of waves. Subtracted terms are shown in gray; active terms in blue. Large coefficients indicate dominant surface error modes.

Profiles tab

Cross-section height profiles through the center of the surface: one horizontal, one vertical. Useful for identifying whether errors are primarily in one direction.

Tips & Troubleshooting

Common issues and how to get the best results.

Getting good fringes
Use a diffuse monochromatic source. Point sources create uneven illumination that degrades the auto-mask.
Clean both the optical flat and the part surface. Dust particles create localized bright spots that confuse the phase extraction.
Adjust the camera exposure so that fringes are clearly visible without clipping (no fully white or fully black regions).
Troubleshooting
No fringes detected: Check that the mask threshold is not too high. Try lowering it to 5-10%.
Noisy surface map: The fringe density may be too high for the camera resolution. Reduce the number of fringes by adjusting the angle of the optical flat.
Phase unwrapping errors: These appear as sharp step discontinuities in the surface map. They occur when fringes are too closely spaced or when the image is noisy. Try increasing the mask threshold to exclude low-quality regions.
Wrong wavelength: If PV/RMS values seem unreasonable, verify the wavelength setting matches your actual light source.

Diagnostics

Inspect the carrier detection and modulation quality.

Carrier summary

After analysis, a carrier summary row appears below the statistics bar showing the detected fringe period (pixels), angle, and confidence level. The confidence dot is green (good, peak ratio >5), amber (fair, 2-5), or red (low, <2). Click the row to open the Diagnostics tab.

FFT magnitude image

Shows the 2D Fourier transform of the interferogram on a log scale. The detected carrier peak is marked with a green crosshair. A clean interferogram shows two bright sideband peaks (the carrier and its conjugate) clearly separated from the DC center. If the wrong peak was selected, click on the correct peak to override and re-analyze.

Modulation map

False-color visualization of fringe contrast across the aperture. Green indicates high modulation (reliable phase extraction), red indicates low modulation (noisy or absent fringes). Masked pixels appear dark gray. Use this to verify that the mask threshold is appropriate and to understand where surface map noise originates.

Manual carrier override

If the auto-detected carrier is wrong (indicated by a low confidence score or unexpected results), click directly on the FFT image to select the correct sideband peak. The analysis re-runs with the forced carrier position. Click Reset to Auto to revert to automatic detection.

Lens Distortion Calibration

Corrects barrel or pincushion distortion from non-telecentric lenses — improves measurement accuracy near image edges.

Why this matters

Regular lenses project through a curved optical path. Points near the image edge appear shifted inward (barrel) or outward (pincushion) relative to their true position. A pixel calibration at the centre will under- or over-measure features near the edges. Telecentric lenses eliminate this by design; this tool compensates for it in software.

How to calibrate
1Freeze an image of something with a repeating equal dimension — evenly-spaced holes, a grid, a ruler, or any feature you can measure multiple times.
2Open the Setup ▾ flyout in the toolbar and click Lens Cal.
3Click two points to measure the dimension at one position. Repeat at 3–8 positions spread across the image — include measurements near the centre, the middle, and the edges.
4The dialog shows the spread (max deviation from mean) before and after correction, and a dot plot of each measurement vs its radial distance from centre. Grey = raw, green = corrected. When the green dots cluster near the horizontal axis, the fit is good.
5Press Apply. The correction is applied directly to the frozen image — all subsequent measurements are automatically corrected.
Tips
You do not need to know the true physical length of what you're measuring — only that all samples represent the same dimension.
Spread after correction above ~1% may indicate k₂ (higher-order) distortion or inconsistent point placement. Try placing points more carefully first.
Apply lens calibration before placing measurement annotations for best accuracy. Re-calibrate if you change the lens, zoom level, or camera position significantly.
Technical note

The model used is first-order radial distortion: a point at distance r from the image centre is corrected by dividing by (1 + k₁r²). k₁ is found by a golden-section search that minimises the variance of corrected lengths across all your samples. The correction is applied by remapping each output pixel to its source in the original distorted image using bilinear interpolation.

Perspective Correction

Corrects tilt and perspective distortion when the camera is not perfectly perpendicular to the part — common when photographing with a phone or handheld camera.

When to use

If you photograph a flat part at an angle (even a slight tilt), parallel edges appear to converge, circles become ellipses, and measurements are systematically skewed. Perspective correction remaps the image as if taken from directly above, restoring accurate geometry before you calibrate and measure.

How to correct
1Freeze the image first.
2Open the Setup ▾ flyout in the toolbar and click Perspective Correct.
3Click the four corners of a known rectangular feature — a reference square, graph paper grid cell, or part edge — in order: TL → TR → BR → BL (top-left first, then clockwise).
4Enter the width and height of the rectangle in mm in the dialog.
5Click Apply. The frozen image is remapped and replaces the original.
Tips
Use graph paper or a calibration grid for best accuracy — a known square gives equal width and height, so one value suffices.
Apply perspective correction before lens calibration. Both corrections modify the frozen image in-place; re-applying one after the other is fine.
The corner markers are numbered on-canvas (1–4 with TL/TR/BR/BL labels). A dashed quadrilateral connects them so you can verify the shape before applying.
Technical note

A full projective homography (DLT, 8 DOF) is computed from the four point correspondences. Each output pixel is backward-mapped to the source image using bilinear interpolation, preserving sharpness. The destination rectangle is scaled to match the perimeter of the source quad, so the output resolution stays close to the input.

Auto-Detection

Automatically finds circles, lines, and arcs in the frozen image.

Circle detection

Uses the Hough circle transform. Adjust Min radius, Max radius, and Threshold sliders before running. Lower the threshold to find fainter circles; raise it to suppress false positives.

Line detection

Runs Canny edge detection followed by a Hough line transform and non-maximum suppression (NMS). Lines closer than the NMS distance and within the angle tolerance are merged, keeping the longest. The bilateral smoothing slider reduces noise on textured surfaces before edge detection.

Partial arc detection

Contour-based detection that discriminates arcs from straight lines using curvature analysis. Useful for EDM wire-cut parts where arcs may be incomplete due to reflections or chamfers.

Elevation

Detected features (shown in orange in the sidebar) are separate from measurements. Click ↑ (elevate) in the sidebar or press U to promote a detected feature to a permanent measurement that appears in CSV, PDF, and DXF exports.

Run detection with the Sub-pixel checkbox enabled for more accurate line/arc positions before elevation.

Sub-pixel Edge Snapping

Locates edges more precisely than individual pixel positions.

When you hover over the frozen image with a measurement tool active, the cursor snaps to the nearest detected edge at sub-pixel precision. An orange crosshair marks the snap position. Hold Alt to bypass snapping.

How it works

A small neighbourhood around the cursor is sampled. A Sobel filter computes the image gradient — the rate of change of brightness — giving a high response at edges and near-zero in flat regions. The pixel with the highest gradient magnitude in the search radius is identified as the edge.

Then the edge is refined to sub-pixel precision along the gradient direction using one of two methods:

MethodHow it worksBest for
ParabolaFits a parabola to the three gradient values around the peak. Sub-pixel offset = −(g₊₁ − g₋₁) / (2(g₊₁ − 2g₀ + g₋₁)). Fast and stable.Sharp, clean edges
GaussianFits a Gaussian by taking the log of the gradient values and solving a linear system. Handles asymmetric or slightly blurred peaks better.Soft edges, textured surfaces

If the peak gradient magnitude is below 20 (out of 255), snapping is suppressed — the edge is considered too faint to snap to reliably.

The snapping radius shrinks as you zoom in, so at high zoom you snap to the finest local edge rather than a distant one.

Edge Detection Pipeline

Preprocessing

Before edge detection the image may be smoothed using a bilateral filter (controlled by the smoothing slider). Bilateral filtering reduces noise while preserving sharp edges better than Gaussian blur — it is especially useful on textured surfaces like turned or ground parts.

Canny edge detection

The Canny algorithm finds edges in several steps: Gaussian smoothing → Sobel gradient computation → non-maximum suppression along gradient directions → hysteresis thresholding (strong edges kept, weak edges kept only if connected to strong ones). The result is a thin, one-pixel-wide edge map.

Line detection (Hough transform)

The Hough line transform votes in a parameter space of (ρ, θ) for every edge point. Peaks in vote space correspond to lines. After detection, non-maximum suppression merges nearby lines with similar angles and positions, keeping the longest representative.

Circle detection (Hough transform)

Similar voting approach in the (cx, cy, r) space. The radius range is constrained by the Min/Max radius sliders to limit search space and false positives.

Partial arc detection

Contours are extracted from the edge map. Each contour is tested against a fitted circle — if the residual error is low and the contour spans at least the minimum arc angle, it is classified as an arc. Nearby collinear contours are classified as lines instead.

DXF Auto-alignment

Aligns the DXF overlay to the image without requiring circle features in the drawing.

Algorithm
  1. Coarse translation: detected line segments are matched to DXF line entities by nearest-neighbour in angle and position space. The centroid offset of matched pairs gives an initial translation.
  2. Angular refinement: small rotation offsets are swept around the initial estimate. Each is scored by a normalised cross-correlation metric between detected and DXF edge positions.
  3. Rotation bias penalty: a small penalty on large rotations prevents over-rotating on symmetric parts where many rotation angles could produce similar scores.
For best results, run line detection before auto-aligning. The algorithm works best when most of the major DXF edges are visible and detected in the image.

Circle & Arc Fitting

3-point circle (Circle tool — 3-Point mode)

Given three points, the unique circumscribed circle is found by intersecting the perpendicular bisectors of two chords. This is an exact solution — there is no fitting involved — so it is sensitive to point placement accuracy. Use Fit Arc for more robust results from many points.

Center+Edge circle (Circle tool — Center+Edge mode)

Click a center point, then click any point on the circumference. The radius is the Euclidean distance between the two clicks. This is exact and requires only two points, but accuracy depends entirely on how precisely the center is known.

Algebraic fit (Fit Arc, ≥3 points)

Minimises an algebraic distance in the space of implicit circle equations. Every point (xᵢ, yᵢ) on a circle satisfies xᵢ² + yᵢ² + Dxᵢ + Eyᵢ + F = 0. The coefficients [D, E, F] are found by solving a linear least-squares system (equivalent to a constrained SVD). This is more robust than geometric fitting and does not require an initial estimate.

RANSAC inlier filtering (guided inspection)

In corridor inspection, edge points are collected and then filtered using a RANSAC-like loop: a random subset is used to fit a candidate line or circle, and only points within a residual threshold are kept as inliers. This rejects outliers from reflections, scratches, and burrs that fall within the corridor.
